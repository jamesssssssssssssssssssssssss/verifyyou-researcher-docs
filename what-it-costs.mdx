A HumanCheck costs about one to two cents per verification. You are charged once per unique human, so a respondent you have already verified never counts again, no matter how many surveys they return through.

That is the whole model. Everything below is just what those two cents look like next to the numbers you already spend.

## A rounding error on sample spend

Line it up against what a complete costs you, and the price disappears.

| Spend | The check on top |
| --- | --- |
| A consumer complete at $3 to $7 | About two cents |
| A $10,000 sample | About $40 |
| A professional or B2B complete at $50 to $100+ | Still about two cents |

Two cents on a $3 to $7 complete. Forty dollars on a ten-thousand-dollar sample. No one calls that a premium.

And it is charged once per human, not once per complete. The first time a real, unique person clears the check, that is the billable moment. When they come back, whether that is a second wave of the same study or a different survey entirely, the [return moment](/how-it-works) is free. You are paying to recognize a person, not to count how many times they show up.

## Compare it to a single bad complete

The real comparison is not the check against zero. It is the check against the thing it removes.

A bad complete, a bot, a duplicate, one person quietly running fifteen identities, costs you $12 to $15 in wasted incentive, and it does the more expensive damage after the fact: it dilutes your data, and it is the reason your client quietly wonders whether the sample was real.

<Note>
Two cents to keep a bad respondent out. $12 to $15 already spent once one gets in, before you count the cost of the decision made on dirty data. The check pays for itself the first time it turns one away.
</Note>

A HumanCheck sits in the screener and stops you from paying that $12 to $15 out in the first place. So the number that actually matters is not cost per check. It is cost per usable complete, and adding a two-cent check that removes fraudulent and duplicate respondents lowers it, not raises it. You spend a little to verify, and in exchange you stop spending on respondents you would have discarded anyway.

## Why it is not KYC money

A full identity check runs a dollar or more per verification, fifty to a hundred times what a HumanCheck costs. The gap is not a discount. It is a different product.

KYC buys you a named, legal identity, and it buys the PII, the storage, the liability, and the friction that come with holding one. A HumanCheck buys you a verdict: a real, unique, live human, and nothing else. You are not paying for an identity, so you are not paying identity prices, and you are not carrying identity risk. It is the trust layer between nothing and KYC, priced like the light check it is.

## What your recruitment platform adds

The two cents is the check. What it costs to *reach* a respondent depends on where you recruit, and that number is not ours.

- **Your own list or a panel you already pay for.** No incremental cost per complete beyond what you already spend. The check is the only line item the HumanCheck adds.
- **Prolific and similar.** A screened-out respondent may carry a small floor payment, minimum GBP 0.10 (about $0.14), fixed at study creation, but it is trivial next to a bad complete landing in your data, and the check is what keeps it out.
- **Panel and sample providers.** You add the HumanCheck as a screener criterion; the check screens out the respondents who would have failed quality anyway, before they ever become a complete you owe an incentive on.

None of this changes the shape of the number. The check is a rounding error; the recruitment cost is whatever you already negotiated.

## Questions teams ask

<AccordionGroup>
<Accordion title="Do I pay again when a respondent comes back?">
No. You are charged once per unique human. A returning respondent, in the same study or a different one, is recognized at the return moment and is not billed again. You pay to know a person, once.
</Accordion>
<Accordion title="What about respondents who fail the check?">
They never become a complete you pay an incentive on, which is the point. The check turns some traffic away, and most of what it turns away is the traffic you did not want.
</Accordion>
<Accordion title="Is the price per complete or per respondent?">
Per unique human verified. It does not scale with survey length, question count, or how many waves you field. One human, one charge.
</Accordion>
<Accordion title="Where do these numbers come from?">
They are the shape of the model, not a rate card. The right per-check number depends on your real volume; the completes and KYC figures are the going market rates researchers work with. We would rather quote against your actual sample spend than have you anchor to a row in a table.
</Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
