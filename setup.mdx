Five steps, two tools, about fifteen minutes. Once per study, no code anywhere.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Create your survey" icon="pen-to-square">
    Build your survey in Alchemer exactly as you normally would, with one difference: leave **page 1 empty**. Your questions start on page 2, and page 1 is where the gate goes.

    **You now have:** a normal Alchemer survey with an empty first page.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Create a verification and paste your survey link" icon="circle-plus">
    In the VerifyYou dashboard: click **New verification**, name the study, and pick **Alchemer** under "Where does your survey live?". That choice is how we know to mint one-time passwords, the credential Alchemer's own gate understands. Paste your Alchemer survey link and click **Create verification**.

    **You now have:** your **study link** (the one you share, your Alchemer link stays private) and your **one-time password list**.

    <Frame caption="Dashboard mockup, a simple screen we are finishing now. What powers it, the hosted check and per-person single-use credentials, is live in the product today.">
      <img src="/images/mock-create.png" alt="VerifyYou new verification screen" />
    </Frame>
  </Step>
  <Step title="Add your passwords to Alchemer" icon="key">
    On **page 1** of your survey, click **Add New: Action**, pick **Login/Password**, and choose **"Individual (one-time) passwords that deactivate after use."** Paste your password list, one per line. Keep this action alone on page 1.

    This is Alchemer's own [Login/Password action](https://help.alchemer.com/help/login-password-action), and that is Alchemer's own wording: each password deactivates after use. Their server enforces the gate before a single question shows.

    **You now have:** a survey that refuses anyone without a valid, unused password.

    <Frame caption="Alchemer's own UI: the one-time password mode, pasted one per line.">
      <img src="/images/alchemer-login-editor-2.png" alt="Alchemer one-time passwords editor" />
    </Frame>
  </Step>
  <Step title="Set the password to fill itself in" icon="wand-magic-sparkles">
    In the same action, open the **Display** tab and set **Pre-populate Password** to the merge code `[url("vypw")]`. Click **Save Action**.

    **You now have:** a gate that opens itself for verified respondents. The password each verified person carries on their link lands in the box on its own, they type nothing.

    <Frame caption="Alchemer's own UI. The example shown uses a contact merge code; yours uses the URL one above.">
      <img src="/images/alchemer-prepopulate.png" alt="Alchemer pre-populate password setting" />
    </Frame>
  </Step>
  <Step title="Share your study link" icon="share-nodes">
    Paste the study link from step 2 into your panel, your sample provider, or your invite email.

    **You now have:** a fielded study where everyone who reaches your survey came through the check. That's the whole setup.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## The fifteen-minute proof

No real study needed. Make up a survey, put the gate in front of it, and try to beat it yourself.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Set it up">Steps 1 through 5 above, on a made-up survey. About fifteen minutes of clicking.</Step>
  <Step title="Try to beat it">Take the survey through the study link; it just works. Try to take it again, and you can't. Try the raw Alchemer URL with no password, and you get nothing.</Step>
  <Step title="Check the ledger">Export the CSV and see the verified column against your response IDs. That comparison, run by you and not by us, is worth more than anything we could say.</Step>
</Steps>

<Note>
**Where this stands, honestly.** Two details we confirm live in the builder during a first test: exactly when Alchemer marks a one-time password as used, and whether the pre-filled login page can skip itself entirely. Worst case, the respondent sees a filled box and taps one button.
</Note>
