There is one moving part. A respondent passes through a HumanCheck before they reach your survey, and out the other side comes a token: a claim ticket only we can mint, that works once, and that the same face cannot earn twice. Drop it in front of any survey. Your survey only ever sees a yes or no.

Nothing about your platform has to change. The check runs on our side, the respondent barely notices it, and what lands on your survey is a verdict, not a face.

## The flow, end to end

<Steps>
  <Step title="You create a gated link">
    Call `POST /v3/initialize` — or grab a no-code share link from the dashboard — with a return path, your survey URL, and any participant identifiers you want carried through. We hand back a URL that looks like `app.verifyyou.com/v/SESSION`. That is the link you paste into your screener, your recruitment ad, or your invitation email. It is the front door; the raw survey URL is not.

    Key parameters: `verification_id` or `verification_external_id`, `origin`, `return_path`, `pass_params` (carries your participant identifiers — a `PROLIFIC_PID`, an `RID` — back on the return URL so your survey reads them without asking again), and optionally `start_path` and `external_tracker` (echoed on webhooks only, not on the return URL).
  </Step>
  <Step title="The respondent does a quick face scan">
    Two checks run back to back on our side, invisible to you and to your survey tool.

    - **Liveness** — is a live person present right now, or is this a replay, a printed photo, a mask, or a generated face? This is the humanness check.
    - **Uniqueness** — have we seen this exact face before in this study? A **collision** means we refuse to mint a second token, no matter how clean the second attempt looks.

    The scan itself takes a few seconds; the whole flow is typically well under a minute. Starting on a desktop? We hand the session to their phone with a QR code and bring them back automatically. The respondent never leaves the hosted flow at `app.verifyyou.com` until both checks are done. Seconds, a camera, no documents.
  </Step>
  <Step title="We route on the result">
    **Verified.** We send the respondent back to your `origin + return_path` with `?vyt=TOKEN&vyc=1` appended. `vyt` is the single-use token we just minted. `vyc=1` is a UI hint only, not proof; confirm it server-side.

    **Not verified** — a bot, a deepfake, a duplicate face, or a decline. We return the respondent to the same `origin + return_path` with `?vyc=0`. Your return page reads `vyc=0` and routes them to your platform's screen-out or disqualification URL.

    <Note>
      A dedicated failure redirect — where we forward a non-verified respondent straight to a screen-out URL on your behalf — does not exist yet. It is on the build list. Until it ships, handle `vyc=0` on your own return page and route the screen-out from there.
    </Note>
  </Step>
  <Step title="Your study confirms the token">
    When a response lands, your survey, your platform, or your own server hands the ticket back to us:

    ```http
    GET /v3/survey/peek?vyt=TOKEN
    Authorization: Bearer pk_val_FLOW_RAND
    ```

    We answer `{ "verified": true, "reason": "ok" }` or `{ "verified": false, "reason": "..." }`. The peek is idempotent and does not burn the token, so you can check at entry and again from a webhook without risk of consuming it prematurely. When you are ready to accept the response as a real complete, burn it once:

    ```http
    POST /v3/survey/consume?vyt=TOKEN
    Authorization: Bearer pk_val_FLOW_RAND
    ```

    We answer `{ "consumed": true }`. After that the token is spent and will not validate again.

    The key in that header is a per-Flow **publishable validation key** (`pk_val_...`). It is validate-only, carries no PII, and cannot create anything, so it is safe to hold in a survey tool's server-side credential store. Always use a key. Calling with no key at all opens the door to token-burn abuse and hands an enumeration oracle to anyone guessing token strings; "no key" is not the safe option.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## What each side ends up with

The point of the whole exchange is an asymmetry: we take on the face, and your study never touches it.

| The respondent | Your study |
| --- | --- |
| A few seconds and a face scan | A verdict: real, unique, live human |
| No documents, no ID upload | A stable id for that human, nothing more |
| Recognized on return, often with zero friction | Never a face, a name, or an image |
| One check, then straight into the survey | One confirmation call, then a clean complete |

We are the trusted broker. The face stops with us; your survey only ever sees a yes or no.

## Two layers, and why you want both

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Layer 1: the gated link (works on any platform)">
    The gated link is enforced entirely on our side, before the respondent ever reaches your survey URL. It behaves identically no matter which survey tool you run, because it asks nothing of the survey tool at all.

    What it turns away:

    - Bots and scripts that cannot pass a live liveness check
    - Deepfake video and replay attacks
    - Repeat-takers — uniqueness is decided at mint time; a collision is detected the moment the same face tries again, not later when you're cleaning data

    The honest limit of Layer 1 alone is the **raw survey URL**. If someone gets your survey's direct link and skips the gated link, they arrive with no token. That is exactly what Layer 2 is for. A check turns some traffic away, and most of what it turns away is the traffic you did not want.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Layer 2: the in-survey token check (depends on your platform)">
    Layer 2 closes the raw-URL gap by making the survey itself refuse or discard anything that arrives without a valid token. How hard it can push depends on what your platform supports. Three tiers:

    - **True gate.** The token is checked server-side before a single question loads; a bad or missing token blocks entry with no bypass. You get this with Qualtrics (single-use personal distribution links with Invitation Only), Alchemer (its Login/Password one-time-password page), MTurk (a custom Qualification), or any wrapper page you host that calls `peek` before rendering the form.
    - **Soft gate plus webhook.** The token routes a bad or missing entry toward a screen-out, and a webhook re-checks every submission the moment it lands and deletes the bad ones through the platform's own API — no CSV cross-referencing after the fact. Typeform, Jotform, Tally, SurveyMonkey, and Google Forms sit here.
    - **Direct call.** The destination's own code calls `peek` before it saves, which is a true gate in practice. Formspark, and any form on a page you control, work this way.

    On soft-gate-plus-webhook platforms the real protection is Layer 1 plus uniqueness. Someone who slips in through a leaked direct URL still cannot earn a second token — the repeat-taker, the pattern researchers care about most, is stopped at the source. You are left with a residual of first-timers who bypassed the wrapper; handle those by not paying for a tokenless complete.

    See [Platform tiers](/platform-tiers) for what each tool supports and how to wire it.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Why uniqueness is the real lock

Liveness answers "is a live human doing this right now?" It does not answer "is this the same person who already did it?" Those are different questions, and the second one is the one that quietly wrecks a panel.

A one-time code that only says "a live human clicked" can be screenshotted, forwarded, or farmed — a group of people each doing one click, each earning a fresh token. A token bound to one unique face cannot be farmed that way. The same person does not get a second token, because a collision is detected the moment their face arrives a second time. The check happens at mint; the repeat-taker is stopped where they start, not caught later in your data.

Think of the token as a claim ticket — a coat-check stub. On a verified pass we generate a random opaque string and record it as: real, unused, for this study, valid for about thirty minutes. It rides along in the URL as `?vyt=`. When your survey, a webhook, or a wrapper page wants to confirm it, it hands the stub back and we answer four questions: did we mint this, is it unused, is it for this study, is it still in time? Only yes to all four counts. It cannot be guessed, because it only matches if we made it. It cannot be earned twice, because one face gets one.

For the full lifecycle and the security properties behind the stub, see [The token](/the-token).
