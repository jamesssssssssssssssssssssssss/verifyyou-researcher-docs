You already screen. You already terminate the wrong people before they cost you a complete. A HumanCheck is one more screener criterion, and it is the one you have quietly wanted the whole time: is this a real person, and are they the only one?

## The trap question, and why it is losing

Every researcher who buys sample already carries a defense. A trap question. A speeder trap. A catch-all attention check buried in the grid. You insert a fake question and hope it filters the respondents who are not really there, the bot filling the form, the person quietly running fifteen sessions for the incentive.

It was always a workaround. The trap catches the careless and misses the deliberate. A determined bad actor reads the trap, answers it correctly, and keeps going. Meanwhile your real respondents hit a nonsense question, wonder what you are testing, and some of them drop. You are annoying the people you want to keep in order to catch a fraction of the people you want gone.

A HumanCheck is the modern replacement. Instead of a fake question you hope catches them, a real check that a bot or a duplicate cannot pass. Liveness first: is a live person present right now, not a photo, a replay, a mask, or a generated face. Then uniqueness: are they new, or has this face already taken your survey under another id. There is no correct answer to memorize and nothing to game. A real respondent barely notices. Everything that is not a real, unique human is turned away before it becomes a line in your data.

<Note>
A check turns some traffic away, and most of what it turns away is the traffic you did not want.
</Note>

## Where it sits in the flow

Put it at the point of value. After your screener questions, before the paid complete. There are two ways to place it: at the front door, as a gated link before question one, or as a mid-survey screener step further into the questionnaire. Either way it is still a screener criterion, it just moves depending on how your study is built.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Respondent enters and answers your screener">
    Age, category use, incidence, whatever qualifies them for this study. The same logic that already terminates the wrong profile.
  </Step>
  <Step title="A qualifying respondent takes the HumanCheck">
    A quick hosted check runs liveness then uniqueness against the pool. A real, unique, live human passes. Anything else terminates here.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Only a verified human reaches the survey">
    Pass, and they continue to the questions you pay for. Fail, and they screen out like any other terminate, before the complete is counted.
  </Step>
</Steps>

This is the whole point of placing it here. The complete is the unit you pay for, roughly $3 to $7 for a consumer complete and $50 to $100 or more for professional and B2B. You do not want to protect the survey after you have already bought the bad respondent. You want to catch them at the screener, where a terminate costs you nothing.

<Note>
The HumanCheck replaces the trap question's job of catching fake and duplicate respondents, but it does not catch speeders or straight-liners. A real, unique human can still rush your survey without paying attention. Keep a speeder or attention check in place for data quality. The two are not redundant: one confirms the person, the other confirms the data.
</Note>

## Start passive: the verified column

You do not have to gate anything to start. A researcher who called us put it plainly: "if I had a column that said verified or not verified, I could compare them." That is the passive flow, and it is the natural first study.

Add the HumanCheck as a step in your survey, mid-questionnaire, the same link-out-and-back you would use for any external task. It carries your session or participant id via `pass_params`, so the respondent leaves for the hosted check, completes it, and returns to the next question. Nothing gates. Nothing gets held. Every respondent finishes the survey regardless of what the check found.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Add the check as a survey step">
    Insert a link out to the hosted check wherever it fits, carrying your session or participant id via `pass_params`. No platform integration and nothing to configure beyond the link itself.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Respondents complete it mid-survey">
    They take the check and continue on to the rest of your questions. Real or not, live or not, they finish and land in your raw export.
  </Step>
  <Step title="After fielding, you get a verified column">
    Join your export against the Activity Log by session or participant id, and you have a verified / not-verified column for every complete.
  </Step>
</Steps>

From there you have two moves. Compare the two groups to see what the unverified respondents were doing to your data, or start paying only for the verified completes.

<Note>
Honest about what this catches: it catches nothing in real time. A bad respondent still finishes your survey and shows up in your raw export exactly like everyone else. The filtering happens after fielding, when you join the verified column in, not during. If you want it to stop bad respondents before they count as a complete, that is the gated flow above, not this one.
</Note>

Today the join comes from the VerifyYou Activity Log, which shows date verified and external id per respondent. A CSV export of the Activity Log is on the build list; until it ships, ask us for the join file and we will get it to you.

This is why passive is the natural first study. Run one study passive, with no gating and nothing to configure, and see your panel's real verified rate. Once you know the number, turn on gating for the studies where it matters.

## Removing the doubt

Everyone buying sample already quietly wonders if it is real. It is the unspoken thing in every panel conversation. You field a survey, the completes come back clean-looking, and somewhere in the back of your mind you are not fully sure a chunk of them were people.

The HumanCheck removes the doubt. Not with a report or a confidence interval, but with a check that ran on every respondent. It changes what you can say to a client. Not "we used a reputable panel and some attention checks." Instead: "I verify every respondent is a real, unique human." That is a sentence a researcher can stand behind, and it is worth more than the doubt it replaces.

The check costs a rounding error to stop the thing that costs you real money and real credibility.

## Questions teams ask

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Does this slow my real respondents down?">
    Barely. The hosted check is a quick moment, and there is no trick question to parse or trap to spring. The lightest check that still protects what is at stake, so your real users barely notice it is there.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Do I have to drop my existing screener logic?">
    No. This adds to it. Your incidence and category questions still terminate the wrong profile. The HumanCheck adds the one criterion your existing questions cannot test: that the person is real and unique.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What does my survey actually receive?">
    A verdict, verified true or false, and a stable id. Never a face, a name, or an image. You confirm the verdict server-side and route the screen-out yourself.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Does it stack with the panel's own quality controls?">
    Yes. They stack, they do not substitute. Panel controls catch what they catch. The HumanCheck adds a real liveness and uniqueness test on top, which is the layer the panel cannot give you.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
